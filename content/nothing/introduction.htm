Yukon First Nations people have survived for thousands of years despite an extreme climate and an uncertain food supply. They hunted woolly mammoths and, after the last ice age, moved on to lands uncovered by melting glaciers. They travelled light, finding all that they needed for food, tools and shelter in the land around them. These people showed a similar resourcefulness in adapting to the many changes of the 19th and 20th centuries, and continue to do so today.

When newcomers moved into the Yukon in the late 1800s, they brought their own concepts of government, education and religion. They occupied and exploited many traditional hunting and fishing sites, displacing the original occupants. Despite these and other hardships, such as introduced diseases and mission schools, Yukon First Nations people found their own path in a changing world. They adopted the new technologies that suited them while keeping strong links to their traditions, language and culture.
