Introduction

Many important political changes took place in the late 20th century. In 1960 and 1961, First Nations people were finally given the right to vote in federal and territorial elections without surrendering their Indian status. In 1973, Yukon First Nation leaders presented Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau with the document, Together Today for Our Children Tomorrow, the first comprehensive land claim submitted to the Canadian government by any native group. The Yukon Land Claim Agreement-in-Principle was signed 20 years later by representatives from the governments of Canada, Yukon and the Council for Yukon Indians. Today, nearly all of the Yukon’s First Nations have signed or are in the process of negotiating final agreements. Aboriginal people are leaders in every order of government: national, territorial, municipal and First Nations.

First Nations people have also experienced a cultural renewal. Elders from all over the Yukon have worked with linguists to record their languages and put them into written form. Young people are working with elders to learn place names, traditional stories and songs, and to regain much of the heritage that was almost lost during the residential school years.
