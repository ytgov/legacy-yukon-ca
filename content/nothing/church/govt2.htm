Government from Within and Without

Most First Nations groups were led by headmen or chiefs. These individuals led hunting expeditions, negotiated with other First Nations and were in charge of trade. People also relied on the experience and wisdom of their elders to learn their place in the world and how to live properly. First Nations people considered themselves to be stewards of the land that supported them and their children. From 1895 on, however, they had to learn new concepts of property, boundaries and sovereignty. By the 1950s, the traditional form of aboriginal governance began to dissolve. At the request of the Canadian Department of Indian Affairs, people began holding elections for a chief and council. The federal government even amalgamated smaller bands to make administration more convenient.

As more and more of their lands were taken over by the newcomers, First Nations leaders fought for the rights of their people. In 1902, Chief Jim Boss (Kashxoot) of the Ta’an Kwach’an, based in the Lake Laberge area, petitioned the Canadian government on behalf of First Nations people. He stated, “Tell the king very hard we want something for our Indians because they take our land and game.”
