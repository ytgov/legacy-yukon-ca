First Nations Church Workers

The Anglican Church owed a great deal to the work of many First Nations catechists and lay workers. For a small stipend, these dedicated people brought the word of God to their people and acted as a bridge for the non-native missionaries by guiding and translating.

Richard Martin
Reverend Richard Martin was ordained in 1926. He was originally from the northern Yukon but settled in Moosehide after losing his eyesight in two separate hunting accidents. He interpreted for ministers who were unable to speak Hän and ministered to the community during those times when there was no resident missionary. When Martin died in 1975 at age 95, the people of Fort McPherson chartered a plane to attend his funeral.

Julius Kendi
Julius Kendi was a Gwich’in Anglican minister ordained by Bishop Stringer in 1918. In the summer of 1915 he was sent to the Mayo district, where he remained for more than 30 years. There he raised his family and ministered to the Northern Tutchone people of the area. According to his daughter Mary, both her parents devoted their lives to the Anglican Church.

YA, Bill Hare fonds, #6799.
Percy Henry
Former chief and “street deacon” Percy Henry has been a strong link between the Tr’ondëk Hwëch’in and the Anglican Church. Through his work preserving and sharing traditional knowledge, as well as his strong spiritual values, Mr. Henry has aided and inspired many of his people.
