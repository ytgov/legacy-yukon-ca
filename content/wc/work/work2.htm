Yukon Women Work Hard
 
To prepare animal hides for clothing, women first laced the hides onto wooden frames. They then used a scraper, lashed to a pole, to remove fat and flesh from the inner surface of the hide. The scraped hide was gradually softened by repeated soakings in a solution of water and animal brains, wringing and stretching. Finally, the skin was smoked over a fire of rotten wood to give it a rich golden colour.

The Daily Grind
Mary Tidd on laundry day at the Ross River RCMP post, ca. 1930.

YA, Claude Tidd fonds, #8533.
Housekeeping in a log cabin with no electricity, no running water, and no nearby grocery store involved a lot of hard work day after day. Then there were the long cold winters and the extreme isolation of remote communities and camps. Still, many women flourished under these circumstances. They enjoyed their independent, outdoor lives and were sustained by their love of the land.
